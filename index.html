MATHS102 Creative Reflection

I left.
Then I came back.

I'm Jotinder Singh, a Computer Science and Statistics student at the University of Auckland. Maths used to feel familiar, then it became something I stepped away from, and now it has come back into my life through calculus.

That return matters to me because it was a choice. My story with maths is not about failure. It is about confidence, distance, frustration, respect, and coming back with a clearer reason. Mathematics has affected me not only as content to learn, but as a way of thinking that keeps shaping how I deal with challenge.

Timeline

The shape of my relationship with maths

In primary school and up to Year 10, I was good at maths. It was one of the subjects where I felt confident and capable. I liked that there was structure to it, and I liked the feeling of working through a problem until it clicked.

That early confidence gave me trust in my own thinking. Maths taught me that patience usually leads somewhere, and that difficult things become less intimidating when I break them into steps. That habit of thinking stayed with me even in the years when I was not formally studying maths.

In Year 11, I chose Statistics over Maths. That was not because I was struggling. It was because Statistics felt more practical and more directly relevant at the time. It was a decision I made on purpose.

After that, I had about a three-year gap from formal maths. Being away from it made it feel less natural, and it changed my attitude toward it. I no longer took mathematical confidence for granted. The gap made maths feel more intimidating, but it also made me realise that leaving something is not the same as being finished with it forever.

Now I am studying Computer Science at the University of Auckland and taking MATHS102. I came back to maths specifically to learn calculus, because it is part of the path my degree requires.

Coming back feels different from doing maths at school. I have less ego about it now and more purpose. I respect it more because I understand why I need it, but also because I now notice what it demands from me: patience, discipline, and the willingness to sit with discomfort until understanding arrives.

Seeing change happen

The graph shows y = x². The glowing dot is a point on the curve. The orange line touching it is the tangent line.

The tangent line shows the direction of the curve at that exact point. Its slope comes from the derivative. For this graph, the derivative is 2x.

I included this because it reflects my life with maths. In the past, (x<0) I was always confident with maths but lost that confidence over time reaching a "0 point" (x=0) which represents my present state in maths. However in the future (x>0) you can see my confidence come back, a "return from zero" state, expanding my knowledge and confidence over time with purpose.

Reputation

Does maths deserve its reputation?

Maths often has a reputation for being cold, difficult, and only meant for certain kinds of people. From my experience, that reputation is only partly deserved. It can be difficult, and after a long gap it can definitely feel intimidating.

But I do not think difficulty is the whole story. Maths can also be creative, satisfying, and surprisingly personal. It shows you how you react when you do not immediately know the answer. For me, that is part of its real effect. It reveals whether I panic, persist, adapt, or grow.

Reflection

What maths has actually done to me

Maths has built patience in me. It taught me that understanding does not always arrive immediately, and that staying with a hard problem matters. That mindset has carried into the way I approach Computer Science too.

It also shaped my degree choice more than I realised. Even when I stepped away from formal maths, I was still drawn toward logical thinking, problem-solving, and structured systems. In that sense, maths influenced the kind of person I was becoming even when I was not actively studying it.

Coming back now feels different because I am not just doing maths because it is on a school timetable. I am doing it with a reason. My relationship with maths includes both wins and pain, but overall I respect it. I chose to return to it, and that choice says a lot about how much it still affects me.

If I answer the assignment question directly, mathematics affects me by shaping my patience, my confidence, my degree path, and the way I respond to difficulty. I left it, but I came back to it more honestly. That return has made my relationship with maths feel less automatic and more meaningful.
